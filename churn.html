The Problem

27% annual churn is costing more than it should

A regional telecom company is losing roughly 1 in 4 customers every year. Acquiring a new customer costs 5–7× more than retaining an existing one, so even a modest improvement in retention has a disproportionate revenue impact.

The retention team currently sends discount offers to all customers flagged as "at risk" by a manual rules-based system — but that system has low precision, wasting budget on customers who weren't going to leave anyway.

The ask: Build a model that identifies the right customers to target, rank them by churn probability, and explain why each customer is at risk — so the team can personalise the intervention.

Process

From raw CSV to scored customer list

01

Data profiling & cleaning

The IBM Telco dataset ships with TotalCharges stored as a string — customers with 0 tenure have a space character instead of a value, which silently becomes NaN on coercion. Fixed by filling with 0. Dropped customerID, encoded the binary target.

02

Exploratory analysis

Churn rate broken down by contract type, payment method, internet service, and tenure band. The single biggest signal jumped out immediately: month-to-month customers churn at 42% vs. 11% for one-year and 3% for two-year contracts.

03

Feature engineering

Created tenure_band, avg_monthly_charge (total charges ÷ tenure), service_count (sum of active add-ons), and a high_value flag for customers in the top quartile of monthly spend.

04

Class imbalance handling

Churn is ~26% of the dataset — imbalanced but not severely. Used SMOTE on the training set only (never on test) to oversample the minority class. Also tested scale_pos_weight in XGBoost directly.

05

Modelling

Baseline Logistic Regression (AUC 0.841) then XGBoost with 300 estimators, depth 5, learning rate 0.05 (AUC 0.847). Evaluated on AUC-ROC and Precision-Recall — not accuracy, which is misleading on imbalanced data.

06

SHAP explainability

TreeExplainer applied to the XGBoost model. Summary plot reveals the top 3 global drivers. Waterfall plots generated for individual high-risk customers so the retention team understands why each person is flagged.

Findings

What drives churn — and what to do about it

Contract type is the dominant signal. Month-to-month customers churn at 42% — 4× the rate of annual contracts. The single highest-ROI retention lever is migrating at-risk short-term customers to a subsidised annual plan.

Tenure is a strong protective factor. Median tenure for churned customers is 10 months vs. 38 months for retained. The first 12 months is the highest-risk window — this is where onboarding investment pays off most.

Monthly charges correlate with churn but in a nuanced way. High-charge customers churn more, but among annual-contract holders, high charges correlate with lower churn — they've committed and derive value. The interaction matters.

Electronic check payers churn at 45% — nearly double credit card payers. This may indicate payment friction or lower engagement. Nudging electronic check customers to auto-pay could reduce churn passively.

Model ROI: targeting the top 20% highest-risk customers with a $10 monthly discount is projected to retain ~247 customers and generate a net benefit of ~$52,000 annually — a 7.4× return on campaign cost.
