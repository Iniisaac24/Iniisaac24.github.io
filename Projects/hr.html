Employee Attrition
Analysis

Identifying who is likely to leave, why, and what leadership can do about it — built on IBM HR data across 4,000 employees.

The Problem

16% annual attrition — $40,000 per replacement

A 4,000-person manufacturing company is losing roughly 640 employees per year. At an estimated $40,000 per replacement (recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity), that's $25.6M annually in attrition costs. The CHRO wants a data-driven answer to three questions: Who is most likely to leave? Why are they leaving? And what interventions are most likely to work?

Outcome

Sales staff working overtime with under 2 years tenure identified as 3× more likely to leave than average. K-Means clustering revealed four distinct employee risk profiles. Three specific HR policy recommendations delivered with estimated cost impact.

Approach

EDA → Classification → Segmentation

01

Attrition Rate Profiling

Computed attrition rate by department, job role, age band, overtime status, job satisfaction score, and years at company. Overtime employees showed the highest single-variable attrition rate at 30.5% vs 10.4% for non-overtime — the strongest binary signal in the dataset.

02

Correlation Analysis

Built a correlation matrix of all numeric features vs the attrition binary. Top 8 correlated variables selected as model candidates: OverTime, MonthlyIncome, YearsAtCompany, JobSatisfaction, WorkLifeBalance, EnvironmentSatisfaction, JobInvolvement, MaritalStatus.

03

Random Forest Classifier

Binary classification — Leave (1) vs Stay (0). Handled class imbalance with class_weight='balanced'. Evaluated with AUC-ROC and Precision-Recall curve. Feature importance extracted to rank the top drivers of attrition across the full workforce.

04

K-Means Employee Segmentation

Used elbow method to find optimal k=4. Clustered employees by risk profile using: overtime flag, tenure, monthly income, job satisfaction, work-life balance score. Each cluster assigned a plain-language label for the HR team.

05

Tableau Public Dashboard

Interactive dashboard with: attrition rate by department bar chart, age × department heatmap, flight risk score distribution, and top 5 attrition drivers. Designed for non-technical HR leadership to explore without assistance.

Code Sample

K-Means segmentation pipeline

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

features = ['OverTime_enc', 'YearsAtCompany', 'MonthlyIncome',
            'JobSatisfaction', 'WorkLifeBalance']

X_cluster = df[features].copy()
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_cluster)

# Elbow method
inertias = []
for k in range(2, 10):
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, n_init=10)
    km.fit(X_scaled)
    inertias.append(km.inertia_)

# Fit final model
km = KMeans(n_clusters=4, random_state=42, n_init=10)
df['risk_segment'] = km.fit_predict(X_scaled)

# Label segments based on cluster centres
segment_labels = {
    0: 'High Flight Risk — Overworked, Low Satisfaction',
    1: 'Stable Core — Tenured, Well Compensated',
    2: 'Growth Seeking — Mid-Tenure, Underpaid',
    3: 'At Risk — New, Low Work-Life Balance'
}
df['segment_label'] = df['risk_segment'].map(segment_labels)

Employee Segments

Four distinct risk profiles

Segment A — High Flight Risk

Overworked, low job satisfaction, below-median income. Attrition rate: 42%. Primarily in Sales and HR. Urgent intervention needed.

Segment B — Stable Core

Tenured 5+ years, above-median compensation, high satisfaction. Attrition rate: 4%. Retain through recognition and career development, not salary.

Segment C — Growth Seeking

Mid-tenure (2–5 years), performing well but underpaid relative to market. Attrition rate: 18%. Most responsive to compensation adjustment.

Segment D — New & At Risk

Under 2 years tenure, poor work-life balance scores. Attrition rate: 28%. Onboarding and manager quality are the key levers here.

Recommendations

Three policy interventions

01
Cap overtime for Sales staff in years 0–2

This single cohort accounts for 34% of all attrition despite being 11% of headcount. A hard overtime cap or additional compensation for overages is estimated to reduce overall attrition by 6–8 percentage points.

02
Introduce a 24-month tenure bonus

The sharpest attrition drop occurs after 24 months on the job. A retention bonus paid at the 24-month mark — even at $2,000 — would cost less than 5% of the replacement cost it prevents per employee retained.

03
Targeted pay review for Segment C employees

Growth-seeking employees (Segment C) are leaving for pay, not culture. A targeted compensation review benchmarking this cohort against market rates — with adjustments where gaps exist — addresses the second largest attrition source at the lowest per-head cost.
