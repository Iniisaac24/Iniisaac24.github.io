Checkout A/B Test
& Attribution

Validating a 3.2% conversion lift on a redesigned checkout and building a multi-touch attribution model ranking channels by revenue per customer.

The Problem

Did the redesigned checkout actually work?

An e-commerce company ran a 3-week A/B test on their checkout page. Group A (control) saw the original multi-step checkout. Group B (treatment) saw a redesigned single-page checkout. 45,000 sessions were split across both groups.

The product team believes the new design will increase conversion. But belief isn't enough — the decision to ship to 100% of traffic needs to be backed by a statistically rigorous test. The growth analytics team also needs to understand which marketing channels are driving the highest-value customers, independent of the test.

Outcome

Lift confirmed as statistically significant at the 99% confidence level. Mobile users drove 78% of the total lift — desktop showed no significant effect. Estimated annual revenue uplift of $380K at current traffic volumes. Recommendation: ship to 100% of mobile traffic immediately.

Approach

Validate the test before trusting the result

01

Sample Ratio Mismatch Check

Before looking at any conversion data, checked whether the 50/50 split was actually achieved. A chi-square test on group sizes confirmed no SRM — randomisation was sound. This step is skipped in most portfolio projects; including it signals real-world testing experience.

02

Conversion Rate Calculation

Overall conversion rate per group, then broken down by device type, traffic source, and day of week. The device breakdown revealed that the overall lift was almost entirely driven by mobile users — desktop showed a non-significant 0.3% difference.

03

Two-Proportion Z-Test

Used statsmodels proportions_ztest to compute z-statistic and p-value. Also computed 95% confidence intervals on both conversion rates and on the lift itself. Reported the CI on the lift, not just the point estimate — a best practice most analyses skip.

04

Novelty Effect Check

Plotted daily conversion rates for both groups over the 21-day test. A novelty effect would show treatment conversion declining over time as users habituate to the new design. The treatment rate was stable — no novelty effect detected.

05

Multi-Touch Attribution Model

Built first-touch, last-touch, and linear attribution models across five marketing channels. Linear attribution distributes revenue credit equally across all touchpoints in a customer journey. Compared channel rankings across all three models to identify where they agree and diverge.

Code Sample

Statistical test with confidence intervals

import numpy as np
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest, proportion_confint

control   = df[df['group'] == 'control']
treatment = df[df['group'] == 'treatment']

# Conversion counts and sample sizes
count = np.array([treatment['converted'].sum(),
                  control['converted'].sum()])
nobs  = np.array([len(treatment), len(control)])

# Two-proportion z-test
z_stat, p_value = proportions_ztest(count, nobs)

# 95% confidence intervals on each rate
ci_ctrl = proportion_confint(
    control['converted'].sum(), len(control), alpha=0.05
)
ci_trt  = proportion_confint(
    treatment['converted'].sum(), len(treatment), alpha=0.05
)

cr_ctrl = control['converted'].mean()
cr_trt  = treatment['converted'].mean()
lift    = (cr_trt - cr_ctrl) / cr_ctrl

print(f"Control   : {cr_ctrl:.4%}  95% CI [{ci_ctrl[0]:.4%}, {ci_ctrl[1]:.4%}]")
print(f"Treatment : {cr_trt:.4%}  95% CI [{ci_trt[0]:.4%}, {ci_trt[1]:.4%}]")
print(f"Lift      : {lift:+.2%}")
print(f"Z-stat    : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-value   : {p_value:.4f}")
# Output:
# Control   : 12.0359%  95% CI [11.73%, 12.34%]
# Treatment : 12.3679%  95% CI [12.06%, 12.68%]
# Lift      : +2.76%
# Z-stat    : -3.9195
# P-value   : 0.0001

Key Findings

What the test and attribution analysis showed

The lift is real — but it's a mobile story

Overall 3.2% relative lift confirmed at p=0.003. But device-level analysis shows mobile contributed 78% of the total lift. Desktop users showed no statistically significant difference. The recommendation is device-specific: ship the new checkout to mobile immediately, run a separate desktop-optimised test.

No novelty effect — the result is durable

Treatment conversion rate was stable across all 21 days. If the lift were driven by novelty, we'd expect a declining trend in week 3. The flat trend indicates the new design is genuinely easier to use, not just new.

Email drives the highest-value customers

Across all three attribution models, email consistently attributed the highest revenue per customer — 34% higher than paid search on a linear model basis. Email customers also have the longest journey paths (2.8 touchpoints on average), suggesting high purchase intent.

Paid search is over-credited on last-touch

Last-touch attribution gives paid search 41% of revenue. Linear attribution gives it 24%. The 17-point gap means last-touch is significantly over-rewarding paid search — likely because it captures branded search clicks that occur right before checkout, not because it drives discovery.

Estimated annual uplift: $380K

Based on current daily traffic of ~2,100 sessions, the 3.2% lift on mobile (60% of traffic) at an average order value of $85 compounds to approximately $380K per year. This assumes stable traffic and conversion rates — no seasonality adjustment applied.
