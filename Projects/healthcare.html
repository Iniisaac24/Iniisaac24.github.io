Approach

From raw encounters to tested associations

01

Clean

Converted the dataset's ? missing-marker to NaN, dropped high-missing columns (weight was 96.9% empty), and collapsed the target to a binary 30-day readmission flag.

02

Group diagnoses

Mapped ICD-9 diag_1 codes into clinical categories (circulatory, respiratory, diabetes, …).

03

Test, don't eyeball

Chi-square for categorical associations and Mann-Whitney U for skewed continuous variables — reporting p-values, not just visual differences.

Findings

What actually predicts readmission — and what doesn't

Prior inpatient visits are the strongest signal (Mann-Whitney p ≈ 0). A patient's history of previous admissions outweighs any single clinical variable — the clearest lever for a discharge-follow-up protocol.

Medication burden matters: insulin use (χ²=190.8, p=4e-41) and total diagnosis count (p=2.8e-51) are strongly associated — both proxies for case complexity.

A1C result is not associated with readmission (p=0.83) — despite being the dataset's namesake variable, and it's missing for 83% of encounters. A commonly-assumed driver the data does not support.

Diagnosis category is significant but weak: readmission rates span only 9.5%–13.0% across groups. Circulatory patients sit at 1.03× the overall rate — not a strong stratifier on its own.

Clear age gradient (χ²=116.6, p=6.5e-21): from 1.9% for children to ~12% for elderly patients.
