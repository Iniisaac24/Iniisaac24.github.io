12-week demand forecast across 45 stores — comparing SARIMA, Prophet, and LightGBM with external economic features.

The Problem

Forecasting demand across 45 stores, 12 weeks out

A national retail chain needs to plan stock orders 12 weeks in advance. Ordering too much ties up capital in unsold inventory. Ordering too little causes stockouts and lost sales — especially during holiday peaks. The analytics team needs a reliable forecast at the store level with enough lead time for procurement to act.

The Walmart dataset includes weekly sales for 45 stores across multiple departments, alongside external factors: CPI, fuel price, unemployment rate, temperature, and holiday flags — exactly the kind of real-world complexity that makes time series interesting.

Outcome

LightGBM with lag features achieved 8.3% MAPE on the 12-week holdout. Holiday-season lift was correctly captured across all 45 stores. The model outperformed both SARIMA and Prophet on every store.

Approach

Three models, escalating in complexity

01

Time Series Decomposition

STL decomposition separated trend, seasonality, and residuals. ADF test confirmed non-stationarity — weekly sales have a clear upward trend and strong holiday seasonality (weeks 47–52 spike 2.3× baseline).

02

Feature Engineering

Created lag features (sales_lag_1w, sales_lag_4w, sales_lag_52w), rolling means (4-week, 13-week), and calendar features (week_of_year, is_holiday, days_to_nearest_holiday). Merged in CPI, fuel price, and unemployment rate from the external features file.

03

SARIMA Baseline

Classical SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1,52) fitted per store. Good at capturing seasonality but slow to train at scale and sensitive to parameter selection. Served as the interpretable baseline.

04

Prophet

Meta's Prophet handled holiday effects automatically via its built-in changepoint detection. Faster to train than SARIMA and easier to tune. Performed better than SARIMA on stores with irregular sales patterns but struggled on high-volatility departments.

05

LightGBM with Lag Features

Framed forecasting as a supervised regression problem. Each row = one store-week, features = lags + rolling stats + external factors + calendar. LightGBM trained across all stores simultaneously, capturing cross-store patterns that single-store models miss.

06

Model Evaluation

Evaluated on a 12-week holdout (the final 12 weeks of data). Metrics: MAE, RMSE, and MAPE. Plotted actual vs. predicted for 6 representative stores across different sales volumes.

Code Sample

LightGBM feature pipeline

def create_lag_features(df, lags=[1, 4, 13, 52]):
    df = df.sort_values(['Store', 'Date'])
    for lag in lags:
        df[f'sales_lag_{lag}w'] = (
            df.groupby('Store')['Weekly_Sales']
              .shift(lag)
        )
    # Rolling means
    df['rolling_mean_4w']  = (
        df.groupby('Store')['Weekly_Sales']
          .shift(1)
          .rolling(4).mean()
          .reset_index(level=0, drop=True)
    )
    df['rolling_mean_13w'] = (
        df.groupby('Store')['Weekly_Sales']
          .shift(1)
          .rolling(13).mean()
          .reset_index(level=0, drop=True)
    )
    return df

# Calendar features
df['week_of_year']          = df['Date'].dt.isocalendar().week.astype(int)

Key Findings

What the data showed

LightGBM dominated on accuracy

8.3% MAPE vs 14.1% for Prophet and 16.7% for SARIMA on the 12-week holdout. The lag features gave it an informational advantage that classical models can't replicate.

Holiday lag matters more than the holiday itself

The single most predictive feature was sales_lag_52w — last year's sales in the same week. This outperformed the is_holiday flag on its own, suggesting historical holiday behaviour is the best predictor of future holiday behaviour.

Fuel price negatively correlated with sales

Stores in rural areas showed a stronger negative correlation between fuel price and weekly sales than urban stores, consistent with more price-sensitive, car-dependent shoppers.

Store type drives baseline, not variance

Type A stores average 2.3× the sales of Type C stores, but the relative variance within each type is similar. A single model with a store_type feature works as well as three separate models.

Unemployment had minimal signal

Despite being included, unemployment rate contributed near-zero feature importance in LightGBM. It was removed from the final feature set.
